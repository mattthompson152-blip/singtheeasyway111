Free Vocal Exercises

Professional piano-accompanied scales covering your full vocal range. Select your voice type and start singing.

⚠️ Important Safety Notice

Your voice is precious. These exercises are designed to help you warm up and develop your vocal range safely, but you are the best judge of what feels right for your voice.

  • Never sing through pain. If anything hurts, stings, or feels uncomfortable — stop immediately.
  • If a note feels too high, do not force it. Skip it or stop the exercise.
  • Stay hydrated. Drink water before, during, and after exercising.
  • Start gently. Always begin with the warm-up before attempting full-range exercises.
  • If your voice feels tired or hoarse after practising, you are pushing too hard. Rest and try again tomorrow.
  • Consult a professional vocal coach if you experience persistent vocal issues.

These exercises are a guide, not a rulebook. Listen to your body and sing smart.

Select Your Voice Type

Choose the range that best matches your voice. Each exercise is tailored to cover your full comfortable range.

Soprano Exercises — Range: C4 to G5

Four exercises covering your full soprano range. Always start with the warm-up.

1. Warm-Up

Gentle 5-note ascending and descending patterns, gradually moving higher. Eases your voice into action without strain.

Range: C4 to G5 (full soprano range with key changes)

Prepares vocal cords, establishes breath support, smooths register transitions.

How to sing: Use a gentle "mm" or "ng" hum. Keep your jaw relaxed and your breath steady. Move smoothly between each note. If any note feels too high, stop and rest. Do not push.

2. Full-Range Major Scale

Complete octave major scales with key changes, covering your entire soprano range from low to high.

Range: C4 to G5 (multiple key changes by minor thirds)

Builds pitch accuracy, develops consistent tone, strengthens full-range connection.

How to sing: Use any vowel ("ah", "ee", "oo"). Keep each note the same length and volume. Breathe before you need to. Sing legato — smoothly connected. If the top notes feel strained, stop. Never force your voice.

3. Arpeggios

Major arpeggios (Do-Mi-Sol-Do) with key changes across your full soprano range. Builds vocal cord coordination and agility.

Range: C4 to G5 (1-3-5-8 pattern with key changes)

Strengthens vocal cord independence, improves pitch leaps, builds power and control.

How to sing: Use "ah" or "oh". Keep the vowel consistent on every note. Each note should be equally loud. Think of your voice as a trumpet hitting clear, strong notes. If the leaps feel too big, hum instead.

4. Cool-Down

Gentle descending patterns to relax your vocal cords and bring your voice back to its resting state.

Range: G5 down to C4 (descending patterns)

Prevents vocal fatigue, reduces hoarseness, promotes vocal recovery.

How to sing: Use a gentle hum ("mm") or "ng". Very soft volume. Let your voice descend naturally without forcing. Imagine sighing the notes out. Your voice should feel relaxed and free by the end.
