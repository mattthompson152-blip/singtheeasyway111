Warm-Up Exercises

Gentle exercises to prepare your voice before singing. Always start here.

1. Five-Tone Warm-Up

The classic singer's warm-up. A gentle ascending and descending pattern that eases your voice into action without strain.

Benefits: Warms up vocal cords gradually, establishes breath support, smooths register transitions.

Do→ Re→ Mi→ Fa→ Sol→ Fa→ Mi→ Re→ Do

How to sing: Sing on a gentle "mm" or "ng" hum. Keep your jaw relaxed and your breath steady. Move smoothly between each note without gaps. Repeat 2-3 times, starting slightly higher each time.

2. Major Scale

The foundation of Western singing. Master this and everything else becomes easier.

Benefits: Builds pitch accuracy, trains interval recognition, develops consistent tone across your range.

Do→ Re→ Mi→ Fa→ Sol→ La→ Ti→ Do↓ Ti↓ La↓ Sol↓ Fa↓ Mi↓ Re↓ Do

How to sing: Use any vowel ("ah", "ee", "oo"). Keep each note the same length and volume. Breathe before you need to. Sing legato (smoothly connected). Start comfortable and repeat a semitone higher each time.

3. Octave Slide (Siren)

A smooth glide from low to high and back, like a siren. Essential for releasing tension.

Benefits: Releases vocal cord tension, connects chest and head voice, improves vocal flexibility.

Low Do→ High Do↓ Low Do

How to sing: Slide on "oo" or "ng" (like the end of "sing"). Don't aim for distinct notes — glide smoothly between them. Imagine a siren sound. Keep it gentle, especially at the top. Do 3-5 times.

Range Building

Exercises to expand your comfortable singing range safely over time.

4. Pentatonic Scale

A five-note scale common in pop, soul, and folk music. Great for finding natural melodic patterns in your voice.

Benefits: Builds confidence with accessible patterns, trains ear for popular music, smooth voice placement.

Do→ Re→ Mi→ Sol→ La→ Do↓ La↓ Sol↓ Mi↓ Re↓ Do

How to sing: Sing on "la" or "na". This scale skips the 4th and 7th degrees, making it very natural-sounding. Move it up by semitones until you reach your comfortable limit. Don't push past comfort.

5. Chromatic Scale

Every semitone step. The ultimate precision drill for pitch accuracy.

Benefits: Perfects pitch accuracy, eliminates gaps between notes, trains smooth register transitions.

Do→ Do#→ Re→ Re#→ Mi→ Fa→ Fa#→ Sol→ Sol#→ La→ La#→ Ti→ Do↓...

How to sing: Use "ee" or "oo" for clarity. Each note must be perfectly even in length and volume. This is harder than it sounds. Start slow. Only increase speed when you can sing it perfectly.

6. Arpeggios — Major & Minor

Jumping between the notes of a chord. Essential for building vocal cord independence.

Benefits: Strengthens vocal cord coordination, improves pitch leaps, builds power and control.

Do→ Mi→ Sol→ High Do↓ Sol↓ Mi↓ Do

How to sing: Use "ah" or "oh". Keep the vowel consistent on every note. Each note should be equally loud. Think of your voice as a trumpet hitting clear, strong notes. Move up by semitones.

Agility & Technique

Exercises to develop speed, precision, and vocal control.

7. Natural Minor Scale

The minor scale has a different emotional quality — moody, soulful, expressive. Essential for contemporary singing.

Benefits: Trains ear for minor keys, develops emotional expression, improves interval accuracy.

Do→ Re→ Me→ Fa→ Sol→ Le→ Te→ Do↓...

How to sing: Sing on "ah" or "eh". Notice the different feel compared to major. The lowered 3rd, 6th and 7th give it that melancholy quality. Essential for soul, R&B, and ballads.

8. Harmonic Minor Scale

The minor scale with a raised 7th. Creates a distinctive, dramatic sound heard in Middle Eastern and Flamenco-influenced music.

Benefits: Expands vocal colour palette, trains larger intervals, develops dramatic expression.

How to sing: Use "ah" or "ee". Pay attention to the larger jump between the 6th and 7th notes. That's the characteristic sound. Keep the top note supported with good breath.

9. 1-3-5-1 Pattern

A classic vocal exercise that strengthens your ability to move between chord tones cleanly and quickly.

Benefits: Builds chord tone awareness, improves vocal agility, strengthens head voice connection.

Do→ Mi→ Sol→ Mi→ Do

How to sing: Best on a lip trill (brrr) or tongue trill (rrr). These keep your voice relaxed while building coordination. Move up through your range smoothly. If the trill breaks, you're pushing too hard.

Cool Down

After singing, cool your voice down gradually. Never finish a session without cooling down.

10. Descending Cool-Down

A gentle descending pattern to relax your vocal cords and bring your voice back to its resting state.

Benefits: Prevents vocal fatigue, reduces post-singing hoarseness, promotes vocal recovery.

High Do↓ Ti↓ La↓ Sol↓ Fa↓ Mi↓ Re↓ Do↓...

How to sing: Use a gentle hum ("mm") or "ng". Very soft volume. Let your voice descend naturally without forcing. Imagine sighing the notes out. Your voice should feel relaxed and free by the end.

How to Use These Exercises

Recommended Practice Structure

Follow this order for a complete vocal workout:

  1. Warm-Up (5-10 min): Start with exercises 1-3. Always begin gently.
  2. Range Building (10-15 min): Move to exercises 4-6. Gradually work higher.
  3. Agility Work (10 min): Exercises 7-9 for speed and precision.
  4. Cool Down (3-5 min): Finish with exercise 10. Never skip this.

Total time: Approximately 30-40 minutes. For a quick warm-up, do exercises 1, 2, and 10 only (about 10 minutes).

Tips for Best Results

  • Stay hydrated — drink water before, during, and after.
  • Don't push — if it hurts, stop. Discomfort means you're doing too much.
  • Breathe properly — support from your diaphragm, not your throat.
  • Be consistent — 20 minutes daily beats 2 hours once a week.
  • Record yourself — you'll hear progress you don't feel.
  • Warm up before singing songs — never go straight into repertoire.
