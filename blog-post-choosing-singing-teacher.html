Red Flags When Choosing a Singing Teacher

Choosing a singing teacher is one of the most important decisions you'll make for your vocal journey. The right coach transforms your voice; the wrong one wastes your time, money, and potentially damages your vocal health. Here are the critical red flags to watch for when selecting a teacher for your online singing lessons or in-person training.

The Vocal Register Test Every Teacher Must Pass

Before committing to any singing teacher, ask them these three fundamental questions:

  • What is chest voice?
  • What is head voice?
  • What is mixed voice?

Their answers reveal everything about their technical knowledge. Here's what you should hear:

Chest voice is called chest voice because the vibrations are predominantly felt within the chest cavity. When you sing in your lower register, place your hand on your chest—you'll feel the vibrations resonating there.

Head voice is called head voice because the vibrations are predominantly felt in the head. As you ascend in pitch, the vibrations shift upward, creating that characteristic sensation in your skull and face.

Mixed voice is called mixed voice because it's a mixture of chest voice and head voice vibrations. It blends the power of chest voice with the ease of head voice, allowing seamless transitions across your entire range.

Red flag alert: If a teacher cannot answer these questions properly—if they give vague responses about "resonance" without explaining the anatomical basis, or if they dismiss these terms as unimportant—avoid them. These are foundational concepts every qualified teacher must understand and articulate clearly.

They Promise Instant Results

Vocal development takes time. Any teacher guaranteeing you'll sound like a professional in weeks is lying. Sustainable vocal improvement requires consistent practice, proper technique, and patience. Be wary of coaches who emphasise shortcuts over fundamentals.

No Assessment of Your Current Voice

A competent teacher begins with evaluation. They listen to your range, identify your vocal breaks, assess your breathing patterns, and understand your goals. Teachers who jump straight into generic exercises without understanding your voice lack the diagnostic skills necessary for effective training.

They Teach Everyone the Same Way

Every voice is unique. A teacher using identical exercises for a classical soprano and a rock tenor demonstrates inflexibility and limited understanding of vocal mechanics. Your lessons should be tailored to your specific voice, genre, and goals.

Dismissal of Vocal Health

If a teacher encourages you to push through pain, strain, or hoarseness, run. Pain while singing indicates incorrect technique and potential damage. Qualified teachers prioritise vocal health, teaching you to recognise fatigue and strain before injury occurs.

No Credentials or Experience

While formal degrees aren't mandatory, your teacher should demonstrate verifiable experience. Look for professional performance credits, student testimonials, or established reputations in the vocal community. Online singing lessons require additional technical competence—ensure your teacher understands how to teach effectively through a screen.

They Can't Explain Why

When you ask why you're doing a particular exercise, your teacher should explain clearly. "Because I said so" isn't pedagogy—it's authoritarianism. Understanding the purpose behind exercises accelerates progress and builds independent musicianship.

Negative or Dismissive Attitude

The best teachers build confidence while challenging you. If a teacher makes you feel small, constantly criticises without constructive direction, or seems disinterested in your progress, they don't deserve your investment.

Making Your Choice

Finding the right singing teacher transforms your vocal journey from frustrating to fulfilling. Trust your instincts—if something feels wrong during trial lessons, keep searching. Your voice deserves expert guidance from someone who truly understands the mechanics of singing.
