Fixing a Nasal Singing Voice

Does your singing voice sound thin, pinched, or like you are singing through your nose? A nasal singing voice is one of the most common issues singers face, but the good news is it is completely fixable. Through professional online singing lessons, you can learn to open up your tone and achieve the rich, resonant sound you have been looking for.

Why Does My Voice Sound Nasal?

Nasality happens when too much air flows through your nasal passages while singing. Your soft palate (the soft tissue at the back of the roof of your mouth) is not lifting properly, allowing sound to escape through your nose instead of resonating in your mouth and throat. This creates that characteristic "twangy" or pinched quality.

The Soft Palate Solution

The key to fixing nasal singing is learning to control your soft palate. Try this: say "hung-ah" and feel the soft palate drop for "hung" then lift for "ah." That lifted position is what you want when singing. Imagine you are about to yawn - that gentle lift opens up your resonating space and directs sound through your mouth rather than your nose.

The "Ng" Test

Sing a comfortable note on "ng" (like the end of "sing"). Now sing the same note on "ah." If the "ah" sounds similar to the "ng" - buzzy and nasal - your soft palate is not lifting enough. Practice moving between "ng" and "ah," making sure the "ah" feels and sounds completely different - open, round, and resonant.

Exercises to Eliminate Nasality

Try singing on "goo" or "gee" - these consonants naturally lift the soft palate. Record yourself singing a phrase first with a nasal sound, then with an open "goo" sound. The difference will be dramatic. Once you feel that open resonance, gradually transfer it to other vowels.

Another effective exercise: pinch your nose while singing. If the sound changes or stops when you pinch, you are singing nasally. Practice until the sound stays the same whether your nose is pinched or open.

When Nasality Is Actually Good

Interestingly, some genres use controlled nasality intentionally - country, pop, and certain R&B styles use it as an stylistic choice. The goal is not to eliminate all nasal resonance but to have control over it. Through online singing lessons, you can learn to choose when to use it and when to avoid it.
