Fixing a Nasal Singing Voice

You've heard it before. That twangy, pinched quality that makes a singer sound like they're singing through their nose. Sometimes it's subtle, just a slight brightness that colors the tone. Other times it's unmistakable, the voice seemingly trapped in the nasal cavity with no escape.

If you've been told you sound nasal when you sing, you're not alone. It's one of the most common vocal issues I address in my online singing lessons. The good news? It's also one of the most fixable.

Let me explain what causes nasal singing and, more importantly, how to correct it.

Understanding Nasality

First, let's clear up a common misconception. Some nasality is natural and desirable. The nasal cavity is a resonating space, and when used correctly, it adds brightness and cutting power to your voice. Think of opera singers who need to project over an orchestra without amplification. They use what's called "mask resonance," which includes some nasal vibration.

The problem occurs when too much sound gets trapped in the nose, or when the soft palate (the soft tissue at the back of the roof of your mouth) doesn't properly direct the airflow.

Here's what happens: when you sing, air should primarily flow through your mouth. Your soft palate acts like a valve, lowering slightly to let some air into your nose when you make nasal sounds like "m," "n," and "ng," but rising to block the nasal passage for most other sounds.

When the soft palate stays too low, or when sound placement is too far forward, excess air and sound escape through your nose. That's when you get the characteristic "nasal" sound.

The Quick Test

Want to know if you're singing nasally? Try this simple test: sing a comfortable note on "ah" and gently pinch your nose closed while sustaining the note.

If the sound doesn't change at all, congratulations, you're not singing nasally. If the sound changes significantly or cuts out entirely, you've got excess nasality to address.

Fixing the Soft Palate

The soft palate is your primary tool for controlling nasality. Here's how to train it:

The Yawn-Sigh: Yawning naturally lifts the soft palate. Practice yawning and feeling that lift at the back of your mouth. Then sigh on "ah" while maintaining that lifted feeling. The sigh should sound open and dark, not bright or twangy.

The "Ng" to "Ah": Sing a note on "ng" (like the end of the word "sing"), feeling the sound buzz in your nose. Then open to "ah" while keeping the sound in the same place. If your soft palate drops properly, the "ah" should have very little nasal quality.
