The Best Microphones for Home Vocal Recording

If you're taking online singing lessons or recording yourself to track progress, you need a decent microphone. Your phone's built-in mic won't capture your voice accurately — and that matters when you're trying to hear your improvement.

After 25+ years in studios and working with recording artists, I've used nearly every vocal mic on the market. Here's my practical guide to choosing the right microphone for home vocal recording, whatever your budget.

What Makes a Good Vocal Mic?

Before recommendations, understand what you're looking for:

  • Frequency response: Vocals live in the midrange (1-4 kHz), but you also want clear highs and controlled lows
  • Polar pattern: Cardioid (heart-shaped) picks up sound from the front while rejecting room noise — ideal for home recording
  • Transient response: How well the mic captures quick sounds like consonants and breath
  • Self-noise: Lower is better — you don't want hiss in quiet passages

USB Microphones (Plug-and-Play)

USB mics are the easiest option — no audio interface needed. Perfect for beginners and casual recording.

Best Budget: Audio-Technica ATR2500x

~£110 / $130

A solid all-rounder that punches above its price. Good clarity, built-in headphone jack for monitoring, and cardioid pattern. Not the most exciting sound, but reliable and honest.

Best for: Beginners, lesson recordings, demo tracks

Best Mid-Range: Shure MV7

~£220 / $250

Based on the legendary SM7B (used on countless hit records), this USB/XLR hybrid offers professional quality in a home-friendly package. Excellent for vocals that need warmth and presence.

Best for: Serious students, podcasters, semi-pro recording

Best Premium USB: Audio-Technica AT2020USB+XP

~£140 / $170

The USB version of a studio classic. Bright, detailed sound with excellent transient response. Handles loud singers well. The standard by which other USB mics are judged.

Best for: Those who want near-pro quality without an interface

XLR Microphones (Professional Setup)

XLR mics require an audio interface but offer superior sound quality and flexibility. If you're serious about recording, this is the route to take.

Best Budget: Audio-Technica AT2020

~£85 / $100 (plus interface)

The XLR version is even better than the USB variant. This is the mic I recommend most often. Clear, detailed, and versatile — sounds great on almost every voice type.

Best for: Entry-level home studios, serious students

Best Mid-Range: Rode NT1-A

~£140 / $170 (plus interface)

Incredibly low self-noise and a smooth, detailed top end. Great for capturing vocal nuances. The "quietest studio microphone" claim is no joke — perfect for subtle performances.

Best for: Detailed vocal work, singer-songwriters

Industry Standard: Shure SM7B

~£350 / $400 (plus interface + preamp)

This is the mic you see in professional studios everywhere. Michael Jackson recorded Thriller on it. Podcasters swear by it. Warm, forgiving, and rejects room noise beautifully. Needs a strong preamp — factor that into cost.

Best for: Professional home studios, untreated rooms

What About Audio Interfaces?

If you go XLR, you need an audio interface to convert the analog signal to digital. Here are reliable options:

  • Budget: Focusrite Scarlett Solo (~£90 / $110)
  • Mid-range: Focusrite Scarlett 2i2 (~£140 / $170)
  • Premium: Universal Audio Volt 2 (~£170 / $190)

Room Treatment Matters More Than You Think

Here's the truth: a £100 mic in a well-treated room beats a £1000 mic in a bad room. Before spending big on gear, address your space:

  • Closets full of clothes: Surprisingly effective — the clothes absorb reflections
  • Blankets behind the mic: Reduce reflections from the wall behind you
  • Sing away from walls: The closer you are to a wall, the more "boxy" your sound
  • Avoid corners: Bass builds up in corners, making your voice sound boomy
Quick tip: Record a test with your phone's voice memo app in different parts of your room. You'll hear the difference room acoustics make. Choose the spot that sounds least "echoey."

My Recommendations by Use Case

For Online Singing Lessons

Any of the USB mics above will work. Your coach needs to hear you clearly, but lesson audio doesn't need studio quality. The ATR2500x is more than sufficient.

For Tracking Your Progress

If you're recording yourself to hear improvement over time, consistency matters more than quality. Pick one mic and stick with it. The AT2020 (USB or XLR) is perfect for this.

For Recording Covers/Originals

If you're posting to YouTube or releasing music, invest properly. The Rode NT1-A or Shure SM7B with a decent interface will give you professional results.

For Voiceover/Audiobooks

Different requirements than singing — you want the Rode NT1-A for its low noise and detail. Voiceover clients notice quality.

The Bottom Line

You don't need to spend a fortune. Start with a decent USB mic, learn to use it well, and upgrade when you hit its limitations. The gear doesn't make the singer — technique does.

That said, if you're serious about singing, a proper microphone is worth the investment. You'll hear details in your voice that phone recordings simply can't capture — details that help you improve faster.
