5 Vocal Warm-Ups Professionals Use Every Day

Published on March 23, 2026 by Matt Thompson

← Back to all articles

Would you run a marathon without stretching? Of course not. Yet every day, singers open their mouths and blast out high notes without a single moment of preparation. Then they wonder why their voices feel tired, their range feels limited, and they can't hit the notes they want.

In 25 years of teaching online singing lessons, I've learned this truth: the warm-up separates amateur singers from professionals. Not talent. Not natural ability. The discipline to prepare the voice before demanding performance.

Why Warming Up Matters

Your vocal cords are muscles—delicate, precise muscles that need blood flow and flexibility to function optimally. A proper warm-up:

  • Increases blood flow to the vocal cords
  • Reduces vocal strain and injury risk
  • Expands your comfortable range
  • Improves vocal coordination and agility
  • Prepares your breath support system

I had a student named Tom who skipped warm-ups for years. He'd jump straight into songs, wondering why his voice felt tight and his high notes disappeared. When he committed to a 10-minute warm-up routine, everything changed. His stamina doubled. His range expanded. His voice felt freer than it had in decades.

Warm-Up #1: Lip Trills (The Ultimate Starter)

If you only do one warm-up, make it this one. Lip trills (also called "lip bubbles" or "motorboat sounds") are the gold standard for vocal warm-ups.

How to do it:

  1. Relax your lips completely
  2. Blow air through them to create a "brrr" sound
  3. Add pitch by making a siren sound from low to high and back
  4. Continue for 1-2 minutes

Why it works: Lip trills create just enough resistance to warm the vocal cords without strain. They encourage breath flow, relax the throat, and gently stretch your range.

Warm-Up #2: Humming (The Gentle Awakening)

Humming is underrated. It's gentle, it's easy, and it activates your vocal cords with minimal pressure.

How to do it:

  1. Close your lips and hum a comfortable pitch
  2. Feel the vibration in your lips and face
  3. Slide up and down your range smoothly
  4. Focus on keeping the sound forward and buzzy

Why it works: Humming creates a semi-occluded vocal tract, which reduces pressure on the vocal cords while still engaging them. It's perfect for early morning or when your voice feels tired.

Warm-Up #3: Sirens on "Ng" (The Range Expander)

The "ng" sound (like the end of the word "sing") is magic for accessing your upper range without strain.

How to do it:

  1. Make the "ng" sound
  2. Slide from your lowest comfortable note to your highest
  3. Don't force—let it glide
  4. Do 5-10 sirens, each one expanding slightly

Why it works: The "ng" shape naturally narrows the vocal tract, which helps the vocal cords thin and stretch for high notes without the squeezing that usually accompanies them.

Warm-Up #4: Tongue Twisters (The Articulation Sharpener)

Your articulators (tongue, lips, jaw) need warming up too. Lazy articulation makes even beautiful tone sound muddy.

Try these:

  • "Unique New York" (repeat 5 times, getting faster)
  • "Red leather, yellow leather" (exaggerate every consonant)
  • "She sells sea shells" (focus on clear "sh" and "s" sounds)

Why it works: Articulation exercises wake up the muscles that shape your words. Clear diction makes your singing more intelligible and professional.

Warm-Up #5: Octave Scales (The Coordination Builder)

Once your voice is humming, it's time to build coordination with scales.

How to do it:

  1. Start on a comfortable note in the middle of your range
  2. Sing "ah" up one octave and back down
  3. Move up by half steps, staying within comfort
  4. Stop before you feel strain

Why it works: Scales build the coordination between breath, vocal cord tension, and resonance that singing requires. They prepare your voice for the demands of actual songs.

Your 10-Minute Pre-Lesson Routine

Before every online singing lesson or practice session, do this:

  • 2 minutes: Lip trills with sirens
  • 2 minutes: Humming slides
  • 3 minutes: Sirens on "ng"
  • 1 minute: Tongue twisters
  • 2 minutes: Octave scales

The professional secret: Tom, my student who doubled his stamina, told me the warm-up routine felt like "wasting time" at first. Now he says it's the most important 10 minutes of his singing day. Every professional singer I know warms up religiously. There's a reason.

When to Skip the Warm-Up

Never. I don't care if you're "just singing for a few minutes" or "only doing easy songs." Your voice deserves preparation every single time. The 10 minutes you "save" by skipping warm-ups cost you hours of vocal fatigue and potential damage.

Treat your voice like the instrument it is. Warm it up. Care for it. And watch it reward you with range, stamina, and beauty you didn't know you had.
