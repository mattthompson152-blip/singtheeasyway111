How to Overcome Stage Fright & Performance Anxiety

Published on March 26, 2026 by Matt Thompson

← Back to all articles

Your hands are shaking. Your mouth is dry. Your heart is hammering so hard you're certain the audience can hear it. You open your mouth to sing, and... nothing. Or worse, a shaky, thin sound that bears no resemblance to your voice in practice.

Stage fright is real, it's powerful, and it has ended more singing careers than bad technique ever will. But here's what I've learned in 25 years of teaching online singing lessons: stage fright isn't a character flaw. It's a physiological response that can be managed, redirected, and even used to fuel better performances.

Understanding the Fear Response

Your body doesn't know the difference between singing on stage and being chased by a predator. That racing heart, those sweaty palms, the shallow breathing—it's all part of the fight-or-flight response. Your sympathetic nervous system is flooding your body with adrenaline, preparing you to run or fight.

The problem? You can't run from a performance, and you can't punch stage fright in the face. But you can learn to work with your body's response rather than against it.

The Singer Who Conquered Her Fear

I had a student named Sophie who would physically shake before performances. Not nervous butterflies—full-body tremors that made holding a microphone impossible. She loved singing but was ready to quit after a disastrous open mic night where her voice disappeared completely.

Six months later, Sophie performed at her sister's wedding—her first performance in front of family and friends. She was still nervous, but she sang beautifully. The difference wasn't that her fear disappeared. It was that she'd learned to sing with the fear rather than through it.

Strategy 1: Reframe the Physical Sensations

Those symptoms you interpret as fear? They're the same physical sensations as excitement. Racing heart, heightened awareness, energy in your body—this is what excitement feels like too.

Instead of thinking "I'm terrified," try thinking "I'm energised and ready to perform." Studies show that simply reframing anxiety as excitement improves performance outcomes. Your body is already in an aroused state—use that energy rather than fighting it.

Strategy 2: Control Your Breathing

When anxiety hits, your breathing becomes shallow and rapid. This sends signals back to your brain that there's danger, creating a feedback loop of increasing panic.

Break the cycle with deliberate, low breathing:

  1. Place your hand on your lower abdomen
  2. Breathe in slowly for 4 counts, feeling your belly expand
  3. Hold gently for 4 counts
  4. Exhale slowly for 6 counts
  5. Repeat 5 times

This activates your parasympathetic nervous system—the "rest and digest" mode that counteracts fight-or-flight.

Strategy 3: Grounding Techniques

Anxiety lives in your head, spinning worst-case scenarios. Grounding brings you back to the present moment:

  • 5-4-3-2-1 method: Name 5 things you see, 4 things you hear, 3 things you can touch, 2 things you smell, 1 thing you taste
  • Feel your feet: Concentrate on the sensation of your feet on the floor
  • Hold something: A water bottle, your microphone, anything that connects you to the physical world

Strategy 4: Preparation is Confidence

Nothing feeds anxiety like uncertainty. When you know your material cold, when you've practiced every transition, when you've sung through the song a hundred times—you've removed variables that anxiety can exploit.

But preparation goes beyond knowing the song:

  • Rehearse the performance: Sing in front of a mirror, record yourself, perform for friends
  • Visit the venue: If possible, stand on the stage before performance day
  • Plan your set: Know exactly what you're singing and in what order
  • Have a backup plan: Know what you'll do if something goes wrong

Strategy 5: Focus on Connection, Not Perfection

The secret: Audiences don't want perfection. They want connection. They want to feel something. When you focus on giving rather than impressing, anxiety loses its power.

Shift your focus from "How do I sound?" to "How do I make them feel?" This outward focus breaks the self-conscious spiral that feeds anxiety.

Strategy 6: Start Small

If the thought of a big performance terrifies you, start smaller:

  • Sing for one trusted friend
  • Perform at a small open mic
  • Join a choir where you're not alone
  • Do online performances first

Each small success builds evidence that you can perform without disaster.

The Day-Of Performance Routine

Here's what I recommend for performance day:

  1. Avoid caffeine — it amplifies anxiety symptoms
  2. Warm up thoroughly — physical preparation reduces mental worry
  3. Arrive early — rushing creates additional stress
  4. Find a quiet space — do your breathing exercises
  5. Remind yourself why you sing — connect to your love of music

When to Seek Professional Help

Sometimes stage fright goes beyond normal nervousness into performance anxiety disorder. If your fear causes you to avoid opportunities you want to take, or if it causes significant distress, consider working with a therapist who specialises in performance anxiety.

Many professional singers work with therapists. It's not a weakness—it's taking your craft seriously.

The Bottom Line

Sophie, my student who shook before performances, still gets nervous. But now she says yes to opportunities instead of hiding from them. She performs regularly, and she's even started writing her own songs to share with audiences.

Stage fright doesn't have to end your singing journey. With the right strategies, you can learn to perform with confidence—nerves and all. Your voice deserves to be heard.
